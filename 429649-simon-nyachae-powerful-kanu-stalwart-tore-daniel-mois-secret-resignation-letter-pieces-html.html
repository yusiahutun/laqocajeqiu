Simon Nyachae: Powerful KANU Stalwart who Tore Daniel Moi's Secret Resignation Letter into Pieces

 Posted on October 3, 2024  |  4 minutes  |  686 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill
  • Daniel Moi succeeded late president Jomo Kenyatta serving as Kenya's longest-serving head of state for 24 years
  • Moi's rise to power was not without ups and downs, as the book ''Moi Cabinets'' by the Kenya Yearbook Editorial Board details
  • Before submitting a resignation letter to Kenyatta, Moi called Nyachae, who, after reaching Moi's office, tore the letter into pieces

Between 1964 and 1978, Jomo Kenyatta served as Kenya's first president before his death.

He was succeeded by the late president Daniel Arap Moi, who served as Kenya's longest-serving head of state (24 years).

Ups and downs

Moi's rise to power was not without ups and downs, as the book ''Moi Cabinets'' by the Kenya Yearbook Editorial Board details.

According to the book, the late president rose to power despite rigorous effort from a group of senior government officials who were determined to change the Constitution to ensure that he did not succeed Kenyatta.

As detailed in the book, the group included Mbiyu Koinange, Kihika Kimani, Isaiah Mathenge and James Mungai, the then Rift Valley police commander.

Moi had dutifully served as vice president under Kenyatta despite apparent tensions with this clique that wanted to oust him and other leaders in Kenyatta's inner circle.

Too much pressure

At some point, the pressure seemed too much, and Moi couldn't bear it.

Former cabinet minister Simon Nyachae is quoted in the book detailing how Moi almost resigned following continuous harassment at some point.

According to Nyachae, in 1975, Moi had even drafted a resignation letter after the police ransacked his private home.

Moi's private residence, according to Nyachae, had been ransacked by police on Mungai's orders.

"His friend Simeon Nyachae recalled how at one point, as the increasingly condescending attitude towards Moi turned to harassment, the VP drafted a letter of resignation. Moi's private offices in the Rift Valley region had been ransacked by the police on Mungai's orders," the book reads in part.

Before submitting the letter, Moi had called Nyachae, who dropped everything and immediately travelled to Nakuru Town to see him.

Nyachae would then serve as a provincial commissioner from 1965 to 1979 in Central Kenya.

Nyachae read the letter and tore it

Nyachae said when he reached Moi's office in Nakuru, he took the letter, read it and tore it to pieces.

"When I got there, I found Moi with the letter of resignation. He was in his office at the Nakuru Oil Milling Company. The only person with him was his remarkable tall, black and bald security aide, Yator Chomber. I took the letter, read it to the end and then tore it to pieces,'' Nyachae is quoted in the book.

He later asked Moi if he thought Kenyatta was aware of the humiliating search.

''Moi answered no, and I told him that was why it would offend Mzee," said Nyachae.

Moi, as indicated took Nyachae's advice; Moi met with Kenyatta and shared his tribulations.

Kenyatta, according to Andrew Morton, the author of Moi's biography, The Making of an African Statesman, Mzee asked Moi who was in charge of the police.

"Mzee asked him, who was in charge of the police?" quoted the book.

From the publication, Moi also served as minister for home affairs, meaning the then police force fell under his docket.

"In essence, Mzee was instructing him to deal decisively with the matter. Had Moi resigned, or had Nyachae not intervened that day, Kenya's history would have been drastically different," the publication reads.

Moi and Nyachae pronounced dead

Mzee Moi was pronounced dead on the early morning of Tuesday, February 4, by medics at The Nairobi Hospital, where he was admitted to the Intensive Care Unit (ICU).
