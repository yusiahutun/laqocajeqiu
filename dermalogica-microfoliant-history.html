The History of the Hero: Dermalogica Daily Microfoliant

 Posted on July 12, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  940 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

Whether you’re a millennial in a metropolitan city, a retiree in the countryside, or a Gen-Z who’s familiarising herself with skincare, chances are, Dermalogica is on your radar. Even if you’ve not used it yourself, you most likely know someone who does, or have seen the brand advertised in the windows of your local salon. That’s because this American skincare giant, found in 1986 by skin therapist Jane Wurwand, is as far-reaching as it is revered; as of 2024, there are 100,000 Dermalogica specialists in more than 100 countries from the UK to Brazil and Korea.

Courtesy of Dermalogica

An archival advertising campaign seen in trade magazines

Thanks to Wurwand’s background, Dermalogica’s ethos has always centred around education. It is, arguably, one of the reasons why Dermalogica has managed to survive and thrive within an increasingly saturated beauty market. The brand’s therapists have always been passionate about its products so news of excellent formulas travelled via word-of-mouth – no easy feat considering this was the pre the early days of the internet, without social media marketing and YouTube videos guiding you through routines.

One game-changing launch that remains a bestseller from its 2001 debut to this day is Dermalogica’s Daily Microfoliant, a super fine powder formula that transforms into a soft exfoliating mousse when cocktailed with water. Last year alone, one Daily Microfoliant is sold every 30 seconds globally.

But the origin story of Daily Microfoliant began around 5000 miles away from its California headquarters. "I was on a teaching trip to Japan and I was really exhausted, so I told the team I would love to go to a traditional onsen (or bath house) before travelling back to the States," Wurwand tells Bazaar from L.A. over Zoom. Wurwand consequently found herself in the spa town of Hakone, at the base of Mount Fuji where she would park herself for five days, meditating, resetting and hoping for new ideas to spark.

Courtesy of Dermalogica

Dermalogica founder, Jane Wurwand

True to Japanese culture, the onsen experience was steeped in intricate, thoughtful rituals and slow living. "On day four, I was taken for a treatment. I didn’t speak a word of Japanese, they didn’t speak English, but this beautiful person sat me down. She had a wooden bowl, a scoop and took small amounts of powder into a ceramic bowl. My skin was damp and she ritualistically rubbed the powder into my skin," Wurwand shares.

As a facialist, Wurwand is no stranger to scrubs and potions but this was an age-old practice that felt new. "My skin felt amazing. As she was removing it area by area, my skin looked amazing." Through fragmented speech and language, Wurwand uncovered the mysterious ingredient. "It was rice, and I thought, it’s quite a labour of love to perform that with rice."

What followed was a call to Diana Howard, the brand’s head of research and development. The Japanese were on to something. "There’s an ingredient in rice called phytic acid and it brightens the skin," explains Wurwand. Coupled with salicylic acid and papaya enzymes, the Dermalogica version works hard to decongest and slough away dullness. But instead of delivering it in creamy or liquid textures as most brands were wont to do at the time, Wurwand desired paying homage to the ritualistic, tactile way it is used in the onsens.

"It has to be a powder. There's something about when it is ritualised. The movements are precise and your awareness is focused. There's something deeply connecting within it to yourself or to the other person giving you the treatment - not just doing it - but giving it because it's the energy. It’s the only time I’ve pulled rank," she laughs.

"I knew we had to lead with education." And so skin therapists were taught on the mechanics of Daily Microfoliant and before long, clients and customers flocked to the glow-giving prowess of the powder. One simply has to dispense a small amount onto the palm, add in some water to create a lather, and massage onto skin before rinsing off.

To ensure the product remains safe and uncontaminated, jar packaging was out of the question. Inspiration came through a dinner table setting. "I picked up the Kraft cheese container and I'm shaking it on some pasta and I said, 'this is it!'", she says of the tubular bottle of Daily Microfoliant.

Ten million units were sold in its first year, and Wurwand chalks it all down to the instant gratification one gets from Daily Microfoliant. Skin feels smoother, looks immediately better, and yes, it is generally safe to use every day thanks to its non-grainy texture (though "make sure it feels silky in your hands, it should not be a paste," cautions Wurwand) plus the addition of soothing ingredients such as colloidal oat and allantoin. The Daily Microfoliant family has also expanded to include coconut milk-infused Daily Milkfoliant for sensitive skin types and Daily Superfoliant with charcoal for mature and oilier complexions.

“There’s a ritual with this product, and it performs. When you get this combination of delight and then the product delivers, that’s when it is a home run," she says. As for how well it does in Japan? "It is always in our top three bestsellers. Perhaps because in Japan they recognise the idea and concept."

Refills are also now available, using 92 per cent less plastic than the original canister, and this year the Foliant range is moving into 75 per cent post-consumer recycled plastic bottles to be kinder to the environment.

A classic that moves with the times, what could be better?
