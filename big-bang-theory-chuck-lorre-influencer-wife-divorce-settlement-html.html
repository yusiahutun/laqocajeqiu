The scandalous relationship has now come back onto everyone's radar amid the news that the director, writer, and producer, 71, had officially settled his divorce from his estranged wife, 38 - and that he had agreed to pay his ex $5 million.

Chuck - who has been hailed as the 'King of Sitcoms' for his creation of some of the biggest shows like Two and a Half Men, Mike & Molly, Young Sheldon, and Big Bang Theory - first met influencer Arielle back in 2014 when he was already a prominent figure in Hollywood and a multi-millionaire.

But Arielle has insisted that she didn't know who he was when they first crossed paths; despite admitting that she was instantly drawn to the way he 'carried himself as very confident and powerful.'

The relationship quickly took over the headlines, thanks not only to their immense age gap, but also thanks to the fact that they came from extremely different worlds.

But while Arielle's journey was definitely contrasting to Hollywood legend Chuck's, that's not to say it wasn't just as impressive.

Before meeting the screenwriter, the Rhode Island-born blonde beauty spent years battling drug and alcohol addiction that she has admitted 'nearly ended her life.'

She turned to partying as a teen after secretly battling severe social anxiety, and after dropping out of college she began on a downward spiral that saw her 'blacking out all the time, doing cocaine, taking Adderall and Xanax, drinking morning to night,' and even getting arrested numerous times.

Things got so bad that she began to suffer from seizures, but after police found her 'face down unresponsive' one day and rushed her to the hospital, she finally decided it was time to get help.

But Arielle didn't let her past problems define her, and after getting sober, she actually used what she went through to launch a wildly successful lifestyle blog and podcast, ultimately transforming herself into a social media sensation with hundreds of thousands of followers and striving to help others who may be struggling.

Arielle, who grew up in Rhode Island with her doctor father and stay-at-home mother, has described her childhood as 'wholesome' and 'idyllic.'

'I don't have the background that some people might associate with someone who turned into a raging drug addict and alcoholic,' she shared during an appearance on the Tandia Talks Money podcast in 2021. 

But on the inside - she was struggling immensely. She said she suffered from extreme social anxiety and often felt 'separated from everyone else'

'I grew up on the east coast, went to private school, my dad was a doctor while my mom stayed home with my brother and I. 

'We had everything but the white picket fence. We had a golden retriever and I drove a Volvo. It was very wholesome.

'I was popular, I did well academically, I was involved in sports and extra curriculars.'

The influencer explained that while she looked like she 'had it all' on the outside, on the inside - she was struggling immensely.

She said she suffered from extreme social anxiety and often felt 'separated from everyone else.'

She also explained that she was fiercely 'ashamed' of her feelings, which forced her to keep them hidden from everyone else.

'There was just something inside of me that made me feel like I was just a little bit separate from everyone else,' she said.

'From the outside looking in, it looked like I had it all. It looked like I was confident and comfortable. 

'But really, I was dying inside my skin, I was so uncomfortable. I was so ashamed of how I felt.

'I [felt like] I didn't have a manual to life. Everyone else seemed like they knew how to act and be but I didn't, and instead of saying that to someone I built up this façade for years.'

From a young age, Ariella said she 'would look for things to make her feel better.' So when she was introduced to alcohol in high school, she thought she had discovered the cure to her problems.  

'The first drink was like a spiritual experience for me,' she recalled.  'It was like all of a sudden I could just breathe and I felt comfortable in my skin and at ease with everyone around me instead of feeling socially awkward.

'I felt confident and it was like from that moment on, I just chased that and needed that [feeling]. 

'Alcohol became my solution to the discomfort and fear and everything that I felt. It also became my primary purpose and all of my ambitions fell away.'

While at first, she said she 'partied socially' like her peers, she explained that her addiction to alcohol severely 'progressed' over the years.

She started doing cocaine when she was only 17, and things soon became 'very dark.'

'It was a lot of fear and hiding who I really was and how I really felt,' she continued. 'I blacked out all the time, I would have to be carried out of parties, I woke up in the hospital a few times.' 

Arielle headed off to Syracuse University after graduating from high school, but dropped out after a only a few months.

She then started 'hanging out with really sketchy people' and had multiple run-ins with the law; she got a DUI and was once arrested for drug possession.

'I tried to go back to school, I was taking classes here and there. I tried to get a job but like I said, alcohol was my primary purpose,' she added of those years of her life. 

Things got so bad that she began to suffer from seizures, but after police found her 'face down unresponsive' one day and rushed her to the hospital she decided it was time to get help

'I couldn't hold down a job or go to school. It didn't matter to me, if I kept drinking none of that mattered.'

She eventually moved out to Los Angeles, where things 'spiraled' even more.

'I was crazy, blacking out all the time, doing cocaine, taking Adderall and Xanax, drinking morning to night,' she revealed. 

In November of 2013, Arielle said she reached her all-time low after her boyfriend broke up with her. 

'When he left me, that kind of pulled the plug out of the drain. He was always there to catch me and not let me hit rock bottom so when he left, it got really, really bad,' she stated. 

'I was in a months-long blackout. Not eating, I didn't leave my house. I would stay in bed and order alcohol [to be delivered]. 

'My neighbor was a drug dealer so he was shooting me with God knows what. I was having seizures, I'd come to and then have a seizure again.'

Eventually, she said her parents had the police do a 'wellness check' on her after she stopped answering her phone - something that ultimately saved her life.

When law enforcement showed up, she said they found her 'face down unresponsive.' 

They rushed her to the hospital, and her parents flew to be by her side, and they were thankfully able to convince her to get help once and for all.

Arielle officially got sober in 2014, at age 28, and is now fiercely honest about what she went through in the hopes that it will help others who may be struggling.

After hitting 'rock bottom' and 'nearly losing her battle against addiction,' Arielle set out on a pursuit to 'live her best life possible' - and she began documenting her wellness journey online.

She began by sharing it on her Instagram account, and after she started to gain some attention on there, she decided to launch a blog, called The Blonde Files, in 2018, followed by a podcast of the same name - and it quickly took off.

'The bottom line is this: when my reason for working out and eating well is to attain something as fleeting as abs, I’m not inclined to be very motivated,' she wrote in one post. 'When my reason is rooted in taking care of myself and attaining optimal health, I tend to be more driven'

In an old post, Arielle explained that her road to becoming healthy wasn't easy, explaining that at first, she had the wrong 'perception' about what being healthy even was.

'I wanted so badly to be "healthy" but there were a few problems with my perception. I thought health was defined by thinness and abs,' she explained.

She admitted that the way she thought she would achieve said body was all wrong too, as she would 'restrict her food intake' and 'exercise like a madwoman.'

'I ate really low carbs, and really high protein [foods], did a lot of cardio, and attempted some random weight exercises with no real direction – and never quite pushed myself to the point of discomfort,' she wrote.

She said she was often 'tired, bloated, and uncomfortable with her body,' adding, 'I had grown and gained so much in sobriety emotionally but I just couldn’t get to a place of peace with myself physically, so I started looking to others for inspiration.'

She then discovered Australian fitness guru Kayla Itsines' Bikini Body Guide routine, which consists of '28-minute energetic, kinetic, and high-intensity interval training sessions.'

After just one week of doing the workouts, Arielle said she was obsessed, and since then, she has 'never looked back.'

'The bottom line is this: when my reason for working out and eating well is to attain something as fleeting as abs (I mean, even when we have them they’re gone at the end of the day), I’m not inclined to be very motivated,' she concluded. 

'When my reason is rooted in taking care of myself and attaining optimal health, I tend to be more driven.

'And whenever I completely veer off track I know what to do to feel good again; its that simple.

'Exploring what works and what doesn’t is part of the fun and makes progress all the more rewarding, so I encourage anyone reading this to get curious and don’t worry so much about right, wrong, good food, bad food, or perfection. It’s different for everybody.'

Nearly a decade on from Arielle's decision to get sober, she is a wildly successful lifestyle, wellness, and health influencer.

Arielle credits her popularity online with helping her to stay sober, explaining, 'Becoming an influencer has been incredibly empowering. It has forced me to confront these fears in real life'

Arielle credits her popularity online with helping her to stay sober. She once told The Skinny Confidential, 'When I look back on my addiction a lot of it had to do with fear - fear of what other people thought of me, fear of not being enough, fear of feeling uncomfortable things, fear of being my true self, fear of other people. 

'Becoming an influencer pretty much requires that I just walk through all of that, which has been incredibly empowering.

'I’m not saying becoming an influencer is the antidote to addiction, but it has forced me to confront these fears in real life.

'Also, people these days don’t want to see perfection. They want to see messiness, flaws, pain, struggles, triumphs, and just real life. 

'I always say alcoholism and addiction and the traumas I went through have become my greatest assets today because they shaped my character but also give me a means to really connect with other people and be my true self. That is invaluable in this line of work.'

 

Her love of plastic surgery: Arielle has been extremely honest about her numerous and 'pricey' cosmetic enhancements, but has denied that she's 'addicted' to going under the knife

Arielle has admitted to getting a nose job, two lip lifts, a brow lift, and two 'profound radio frequency' treatment on her face. She's seen left in 2017 and right recently

Along with being extremely open about her past battle with drug and alcohol addiction and her fitness journey, Arielle has also been very outspoken about her love of plastic surgery.

Back in March 2020, she spoke out about all the cosmetic procedures she had received over the years during an episode of her podcast.

At the time, she explained that her longing to go under the knife began when she was only 12, since she 'always hated her nose' for as long as she could remember.

So after getting sober, she decided 'it was time' and started talking to a plastic surgeon in LA.

During their consultation, she also learned about lip lifts - a procedure that 'removes excess skin between the bottom of your nose and upper lip and lifts your mouth into position,' per Cleveland Clinic - and after spending years of using 'lip filler,' she decided to get that too.

'At this point I was doing lip filler but I wasn't achieving the look I wanted, so I figured if I was going under for a nose job I might as well do a lip lift too,' she explained.

'Lip lifts blow filler out of the water. They make the face look so youthful and fresh.'

She also admitted to receiving a 'profound radio frequency' treatment on her face, which according to Dr. Yael Halaas, 'uses radiofrequency energy, sent via tiny needles, to heat the skin to the perfect temperature in order to plump up the dermis for a more youthful appearance.'

'I got minor tweaks that really just refined my appearance a little bit,' Arielle (seen in 2017) said of the work she has gotten done. 'Everything I did was to just lift things a little'

Arielle addressed rumors that she had become 'addicted' to plastic surgery, insisting, 'When I get something done and my motives are good and my expectations are realistic'

A few years later, Arielle said she went under the knife again to get a brow lift.

'I've always had hooded eyes, which never bothered me until I turned 30 and things started falling,' she explained. 

'Things start to change [when you turn 30]. No amount of Botox or filler were lifting my brows enough. I didn't want to look sad or tired anymore.'

She got an upper blepharoplasty and an endoscopic brow lift, which involves doctors making 'tiny incisions in the scalp and going in with scopes to lift the brows,' resulting in a 'reduction of the eyelid skin.'

She also got another profound radio frequency treatment at the same time.

But she admitted that the recovery was 'so painful' and frustrating, because it felt like 'nobody noticed' the differences.

'It just goes to show how much more aware we are of things we perceive as flaws than other people,' she continued. 

'I thought, "God my eyes are so hooded it's the first thing people think about when they see me. This is gonna make such a huge difference." 

'And nobody said anything. We are so much more critical on ourselves than other people are.'

In the end, however, she said she was happy with the results because she decided she'd rather the changes be 'subtle' than drastic.

'I got minor tweaks that really just refined my appearance a little bit,' she continued.  'I didn't feel like a stranger when looking in the mirror. It actually made me feel like myself again.'

She added: 'I don't go into a procedure thinking it will make me happier or change my life or anything. I do small tweaks over time as things come up to age the way I want to age'

The last procedure she got was a second lip lift. She concluded, '[None of it] was to change my appearance. Everything I did was to just lift things a little and refine my features.'

During a Q&A in March 2023 on her podcast, Arielle addressed rumors that she had become 'addicted' to plastic surgery.

She replied to a fan who asked her if she was 'worried' that her love of changing her appearance had 'replaced' her 'drug and alcohol' dependence.

'No, I don't believe I have replaced one behavior with another and here's why,' she said. 

'The drug and alcohol addiction was all consuming for me. It was a complete physical and mental obsession.

'It allowed me to escape reality and become numb. The only way I could function was if I was drinking and using. 

'When I got sober I learned that the drinking and drugs were a symptom of something much deeper, a symptom of emotional and mental issues, problems with my thinking and my perceptions, completely spiritual bankruptcy, and the inability to self-regulate.

'I had to learn to deal with all of that during sobriety. And for the last nine years, I look at what my motivations are daily, it's something I have to do to maintain my recovery.

'When it comes to getting procedures, yes it can be a slippery slope but I feel like I'm always in a good place when I get something done and my motives are good and my expectations are realistic. 

'I don't go into a procedure thinking it will make me happier or change my life or anything. I do small tweaks over time as things come up to age the way I want to age.'

And while she was attracted to him immediately, she said she was hesitant about dating him because of their immense age gap. 

Arielle explained that they were friends for 'a year' before they finally got together, and that Chuck was terrified to 'make a move' on her because he 'didn't want to look creepy.'

She also admitted that she felt uncomfortable when she realized she was falling for him because he was nearly 'twice her age' - but eventually embraced it after 'processing the feelings in therapy.'

'Pursuing the relationship was more important to us than what people would think,' she added.

On an episode of her podcast in September 2021, she also spoke about how she handled dating someone in the public eye, and she admitted that she wanted nothing to do with his fame at first.

'I tried to keep it really separate at first, I didn't go to any of the tapings of his shows,' she revealed.

'You can get really dazzled by the production of the shows and being around the actors, I really didn't want to see that part of his life because outside of that, he's such a normal guy.
